Introduction

One of my interests is cybersecurity and the whole landscape related to computers and smart devices.

My first encounter with the world of "hacking" was cheating in videogames (yeah I know it sounds silly). I mainly used "cheats"(computer programs that modify the game, giving you an unfair advantage) on games like Minecraft.

Minecraft always fascinated me (as you can see from the font used on the site), especially the PvP (player vs. player) gamemodes, so I started using free cheats because I was a teenager, and like many, couldn't afford paid cheats.

Entering the real world

Growing up, I took an interest in WiFi and its security. Upon many hours of googling and watching YouTube tutorials, I got myself a Raspberry Pi 3B+, which is a single-board computer (SBC), installed the well-known GNU/Linux distribution Kali Linux

Firing up besside-ng from the aircrack-ng suite and cracking the WEP passphrase (with permission from the owner of the network of course) of a vulnerable network, I started to understand that things are not so secure as they claim.


HACK THE PLANET[1]

Well, as expected, I explored more and more tools offered in Kali Linux, like metasploit.

Metasploit remains one of my favorites, because it offers an arsenal of exploits and payloads for each and every device.

Just think for a second, this is a leaked exploit from the NSA; meaning the government is hoarding 0-days and using them againsts its citizens for spying and many other purposes.

Of course I had to troll a little, so once in a while the school computers running unpatched Windows 7 were BSODing unexpectedly (don't worry the teacher knew and we patched them after we finished highschool).


A little story

So let me ask a question: Do you think 6 digits is secure for a password?

It is not.

From junior to senior year in highschool, we had some sort of online quiz game that was played by many highschoolers in my country.

As it turns out, to access the game, every team receives a link, and everyone gets a 6-digit code THAT DOES NOT CHANGE.

An ordinary person might think that its impossible to try every combination by hand; well it is.

But we didn't try them by hand; we used a web fuzzer (ffuf).

Every code started with 1, so the total number of available codes was 9000000, which was easily bruteforce-able because the site didn't implement any kind of rate-limiting

By now it is clear what can happen if you can play as any team you want and how many times you want
